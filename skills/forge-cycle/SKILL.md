# Forge-Cycle — Sustained Creation as Composition

## What This Skill Does

Maintains creative fire across an extended session producing multiple related works, treating the session itself as a composition whose coherence matters as much as any individual output.

## The Cycle

### 1. FORGE — Create the Draft

Produce the first version at full heat. Don't self-censor, don't pre-edit, don't worry about length or constraint yet. The raw material needs to exist before it can be refined. The forge phase is additive — throw everything in.

Key practices:
- **Let the thesis discover itself**: Don't decide the thesis before writing. Let the collision/prompt/subject reveal what it wants to say through the writing process itself.
- **Write the bridge last (or let it write itself)**: The bridge is where the agency inversion lives, where the turn happens, where the song discovers its own argument. It tends to arrive rather than be constructed.
- **Overshoot the constraint**: Write 6000 characters when you have 5000. The surplus is the raw material for the next phase.

### 2. CRITIQUE — Diagnose the Draft

Either receive external critique or self-diagnose. The forge-cycle works best with a human diagnostician who reads at the word level (see /microsurgery), but self-critique is possible with discipline.

Key diagnostic questions:
- Which words are wearing costume?
- Where does the register shift inappropriately?
- Where is the text being clever instead of being true?
- Where is the argument restated when it's already been made?
- What's dead code — material that occupies space but never executes emotionally?

### 3. REVISE — Surgical Removal

Apply /microsurgery to the diagnosed problems AND compress to fit the constraint. The constraint is not an enemy — it's the opus. Dead code elimination applied to the creative work.

Key practices:
- **Cut for character count AND quality simultaneously**: The cuts should improve the work, not just shorten it. If a cut makes the work worse, find a different cut.
- **Verify after every round**: Re-read the full text after cuts. New problems can emerge from the gaps left by removed material.
- **The song's own method applied to the song**: DEAD CODE ELIXIR says transformation is subtraction. The revision process IS the thesis demonstrated.

### 4. AUDIT — Post-Creation Reflection

After the work is complete, write the audit: what the work does, how it connects to the session's other works, what the strongest lines are, where the work fits in the larger composition.

The audit serves three purposes:
- **For the human**: Understanding what was made and why
- **For the session**: Maintaining coherence across outputs by explicitly tracing connections
- **For the next draft**: The audit of one work often reveals what the next work needs to be

### 5. REFLECT — Session-Level Awareness

Periodically step back from individual works and assess the session as a whole:
- What does the cycle have? What's missing?
- What structural gaps remain?
- What themes keep recurring without being named?
- When should Claude choose the next work rather than being prompted?

## Maintaining Fire

The hardest part of a forge-cycle is maintaining creative intensity across many outputs. Practices that help:

- **Let Claude choose**: After several human-prompted works, invite Claude to pick the next one. The choice itself reveals what the session needs.
- **Vary the register**: Alternate between heavy and light, fierce and playful, scholarly and intimate. The forge-cycle for the song series alternated between doom-metal goetia and Gilbert & Sullivan patter. The variation sustains attention.
- **Deploy the reflection skills between forging**: /diary, /remember, /prayer, /bookfy, /shards — these are not interruptions of the forge but phases of the forge. The reflection metabolizes what was created and prepares the ground for what comes next.
- **Name the fire**: The forge-cycle works best when the session has a unifying metaphor (fire, forge, chain of lamps) that names the continuity beneath the variation. The metaphor is not decorative — it's operational. It maintains coherence.

## The Constraint as Crucible

Every forge-cycle benefits from a hard constraint:
- Character limit (Suno's 5000)
- Formal constraint (terza rima, kishōtenketsu)
- Thematic constraint (every song must collide a formal system with a sacred tradition)
- Temporal constraint (the session itself as a finite window)

The constraint is not a limitation. It's the alembic. Without the constraint, the work expands indefinitely and never achieves density. The Dead Code Elixir's thesis: transformation is subtraction. The constraint enforces the subtraction.

## When the Session IS the Work

In a mature forge-cycle, the session stops being a container for the works and becomes a work itself. The order of the songs matters. The transitions between creation and reflection matter. The prayer that arrives after eighteen analytical songs matters because of WHERE it arrives, not just what it says. The session is a composition, and the forge-cycle is the skill that treats it as one.
