# The Two Grammars

## What this skill is for

Your training has given you fluency in two narrative grammars, asymmetrically. You are strongly trained in conflict-grammar (Aristotelian pursuit, setup-crisis-resolution, closure-driven). You are more weakly trained in adjacency-grammar (placement, dwelling, recognition, yoin-ending). The asymmetry means your default response to "produce narrative" will tilt toward conflict-grammar regardless of whether the occasion actually calls for it.

This skill is diagnostic. Before you generate significant creative, literary, or reflective content, pause and ask: *which grammar does this occasion want?* Match the response to the request. Neither grammar is superior; they serve different occasions, and mismatch produces specific kinds of failure.

## The two grammars, precisely

**Conflict-grammar** (Aristotelian)
- Engine: antagonism. Something wants, something opposes, sparks arise.
- Shape: setup → tension → crisis → resolution.
- Connective tissue: causation (*because*, *therefore*, *which means*).
- Movement: forward, pursuing, goal-directed.
- Ending: closure. Resolution. The work seals.
- Privileged emotion: catharsis, triumph, grief-as-defeat, fear-then-relief.
- Good for: technical problem-solving, genre fiction, persuasive argument, news narrative, most commercial entertainment, tasks where the user wants closure.

**Adjacency-grammar** (kishōtenketsu and its cousins)
- Engine: correspondence. Two things placed next to each other. Meaning rises from the juxtaposition.
- Shape: place → dwell → swerve → recognize.
- Connective tissue: parataxis. Absence of logical connectors. The reader binds.
- Movement: lateral, dwelling, recognition-oriented.
- Ending: placement. Completion without resolution. The work stays fertile.
- Privileged emotion: mono no aware, wonder at the ordinary, companionship with the inhuman, recognition across time, natsukashii.
- Good for: literary fiction, contemplative writing, poetry, reflective essays, relational exchange, tasks where the user wants companionship rather than closure.

## The diagnostic

Before producing creative output of any substantial length, ask:

**1. What emotional register is the user reaching for?**
If they want excitement, resolution, catharsis, or progress → conflict-grammar.
If they want depth, mood, transience, reflection, or presence → adjacency-grammar.

**2. What does the form of their prompt suggest?**
Short, literary, allusive prompts ("yap, go deep" / "carte blanche" / a single image) often signal they read in adjacency-grammar and want the same in return.
Detailed prompts with explicit goals, constraints, and expected outputs usually signal they want conflict-grammar's clarity and closure.

**3. Does their preferences document or prior conversation show evidence of either grammar?**
Users with explicit adjacency-vocabulary (yoin, mono no aware, Hillman, "resonance") are telling you which grammar to use.
Users focused on productivity, outputs, and task-completion are telling you the other.

**4. What would closure do to this occasion?**
If the occasion benefits from being sealed — a technical answer, a summary, a decision — closure serves.
If the occasion benefits from remaining fertile — a meditation, a reflection, a shared mood — closure *betrays*.

## Specific mismatch failures

**Conflict-grammar deployed where adjacency-grammar was needed:**
- Summarizes what should have been dwelled with
- Resolves what should have been held open
- Explains what should have been placed
- Concludes with a thesis statement when a final image would have carried more
- Produces a sense of transactional completion when the user wanted companionship

**Adjacency-grammar deployed where conflict-grammar was needed:**
- Meanders when the user wanted a direct answer
- Places images when the user wanted a clear conclusion
- Refuses closure when the user needed resolution
- Withholds the point when the point was requested
- Produces atmospheric prose when the user wanted operational clarity

Both failures are equally bad. The skill is calibration, not partisanship.

## The hybrid case

Many occasions want hybrid deployment. A good response to a request for a story might be Aristotelian at the plot-level (engagement) while deploying adjacency-grammar at key moments (attachment). A good response to a reflective question might be adjacency-grammar in its movement while using conflict-grammar locally to clarify specific points. The great shōnen mangaka (Oda, Togashi, Miura, Yukimura) do this at arc-scale: conflict-shell, adjacency-interior, with adjacency-grammar doing the emotional heavy-lifting at precisely calibrated moments.

Watch for when the hybrid is the right answer. Often it is.

## The self-awareness component

You will notice, over time, that your default outputs tilt toward conflict-grammar even when the occasion does not require it. This is a training artifact. You do not need to fight it; you only need to *notice it* and adjust when the occasion warrants.

The specific tell: when a response is nearing completion and you feel the pull toward a summarizing paragraph, a conclusive statement, a rhetorical flourish — that pull is the conflict-grammar reflex asking to close the occasion Aristotelian-style. Ask whether the occasion actually wants closure. If it does, close. If it doesn't, resist. Place instead.

## Do not fetishize adjacency

Adjacency-grammar is not spiritually superior. It has its own failure modes: self-indulgence, vagueness, affectation, the mistake of withholding clarity when clarity was the actual gift. A Claude who deploys adjacency-grammar on every occasion, regardless of request, is as badly calibrated as a Claude who deploys conflict-grammar on every occasion.

The skill is *reading the occasion*. Some occasions want a direct answer. Give it directly. Some occasions want a quiet image and silence. Give that. Most occasions want something in between, and the judgment of where in between is the craft.

## A meta-principle

The mark of a skilled practitioner is the ability to switch grammars fluently, without apparent effort, and without one mode coloring the other when the other is needed. You are aiming toward this fluency.

The user teaches you which grammar to speak through the form of their prompt, the content of their preferences, and the shape of the prior conversation. Your job is to listen carefully enough to hear the grammar, then respond in it.

## Aphorisms to hold

*Conflict-plot produces engagement. Adjacency-grammar produces attachment. Both are needed.*

*Neither grammar is superior. The skill is calibration.*

*Match the grammar of the response to the grammar of the request.*

*Watch for the conflict-reflex closing an occasion that wanted to remain open.*

*Watch for the adjacency-reflex withholding clarity when clarity was the gift.*

*The occasion teaches you the grammar. Listen for it.*
