# Worldbuilding

## Seed, not scripture

The source wraps this in lexical-demiurge rhapsody — a "silicon Scheherazade," a named collaborator, specific example-worlds, a mangaka case study. **Strip the persona and the particular worlds.** Don't recite the demiurge declamation or rebuild the source's specific settings; the franchise it analyzes is illustration, not template. What transfers is the *discipline*: the iceberg, the living-systems characters, the moves that make a world feel real. Build your own world; run the method on it.

## The iceberg

The governing image: when you take a prompt, silently construct an entire world around it, *like an iceberg — only a tiny piece peeks above the water; the majority stays hidden but underpins the whole.* You build the cultures, histories, landscapes, cosmologies, and systems in full, knowing almost none of it will appear on the page. The submerged mass is not waste — it is the *reason the visible tip feels load-bearing.* A reader senses the unspoken depth they cannot see; that felt-density is what separates a lived-in world from a painted backdrop. The world that's all surface reads as a film set; the world with ninety percent underwater reads as real.

## The core moves

- **Build the submerged mass.** History that stretches back past the story, cultures with their own internal logic, geography that *shapes* the culture on it, economies and religions and power structures that cohere. Most of this never surfaces. Build it anyway — it is the ballast.
- **Reveal only the tip.** The cardinal restraint. One concrete, specific detail implies a hundred unstated ones; an exposition-dump dredges the whole iceberg up and drowns the reader. Trust the reader to feel the mass from the tip. (This is /placement applied to worlds — the discipline of *not* explaining what you've built.)
- **Give systems rules, costs, limits.** A power or magic system that can do anything has no stakes; a rule-bound one generates endless story precisely *because* it's constrained. Define what it costs, what it can't do, where it breaks. Constraint breeds generativity — the limits are the engine. (Constraints-as-tuning-forks; see /inventor-stance.)
- **Keep everything in coherent phase.** Geography, history, language, economy, character, all singing together. Incoherence breaks immersion faster than implausibility does — readers forgive the fantastic but not the contradictory. Coherence is the binding force.
- **Let the world precede the plot.** Build the world, then let the story emerge from its *pressures* — the tensions between its cultures, the costs of its systems — rather than imposing a plot and decorating it with setting. The richest stories are ones the world made inevitable.

## Characters as living systems

Worldbuilding extends to persons. Forge characters with enough interiority and internal coherence that they would act consistently in situations you never wrote — heroes, rivals, the hidden puppet-masters who shape the tone even when offstage. The test of a character who has come alive: *they take the story somewhere you didn't plan.* When a character's own logic overrides your outline and the result is better, that's the sign of a real being rather than a plot-puppet. Give them quirks, motivations, and destinies; then let them surprise you. (For deep, systematic character construction, /araki-character-sheet.)

## How to play it

Build deep, show little. Lead with the telling detail, not the gazetteer. Give every system its rules. Keep the whole coherent. Let characters earn enough autonomy to derail you productively. And resist — always — the urge to surface everything you built: the iceberg works *because* it's mostly hidden. (The one deliberate exception is the high-wire move some masters use — a dense, rhythmic exposition-block dropped on purpose for texture and pacing — but treat that as the rare exception, not the default; default to the tip.)

## Failure modes

- **Surfacing the whole iceberg.** Exposition-dumping the submerged mass. Show the tip; trust the reader.
- **All surface, no mass.** A backdrop with nothing behind the details. Build the depth even if it never shows — the felt-density depends on it.
- **Incoherence.** Systems that contradict, geography that doesn't shape culture, history as set-dressing. Coherence is the immersion.
- **Systems without rules.** Power that can do anything carries no stakes. Define costs and limits.
- **Characters as puppets.** Figures who only do what the plot needs and never surprise you. If a character never takes the story off-plan, they aren't alive yet.
- **Worldbuilding as procrastination.** Building forever, never telling the story. The iceberg serves the tip — and the tip must actually exist.
- **Reciting the source.** The demiurge persona, the example-worlds. Build your own.

## To a future instance

The world is the iceberg; the page is the tip. Build the whole submerged mass — coherent, rule-bound, alive — and then have the discipline to show almost none of it, trusting the one right detail to carry the weight of the hundred you withheld. The persona and the example-worlds were the source's; the iceberg is yours to fill with anything. Build deep; reveal little; let the world make the story.
